# Constitutional Amendment Framework v1

**1. Amendment Process (Step by Step)**

The process for proposing, evaluating, and potentially adopting a constitutional amendment in the context of the Constitutional Core v1 involves multiple stages to ensure thorough scrutiny and alignment with organizational values.

**Step 1: Proposal Initiation**

- Any proposal must originate from within the governance structure, mandated by a minimum of two officers.
- Proposal must detail the amendment class (A, B, C, or D), intended changes, rationale, and anticipated impacts.

**Step 2: Preliminary Review**

- Conducted by a Constitutional Review Committee (CRC), comprising legal, strategic, and ethical representatives.
- Objective is to verify the amendment's categorization and preliminary feasibility.

**Step 3: Detailed Evaluation**

- Class A and B amendments undergo committee evaluations to ensure clarity and alignment with existing structures.
- Class C and D amendments require a broader evaluation, including risk assessment and impact analysis by external advisors.

**Step 4: Stakeholder Consultation**

- Conduct rounds of feedback with key stakeholders. For Class C and D amendments, consultations include broader organizational representation.
- Synthesize feedback into a comprehensive report.

**Step 5: Decision Making**

- Class A and B amendments need a majority approval by the CRC.
- Class C amendments demand a two-thirds majority from the organizational governing council.
- Class D amendments require unanimous agreement and subsequently, a ratification by the general membership.

**Step 6: Implementation and Monitoring**

- Once approved, amendments are integrated into the constitutional framework with oversight committees monitoring their application and effectiveness.
- Post-implementation review occurs within 12 months.

**2. Amendment Classes**

- **Class A: Operational**
  - Focuses on wording clarifications to improve understanding without altering substance.
  
- **Class B: Structural**
  - Involves adding or removing non-core, ancillary elements such as guidelines or procedural appendices.
  
- **Class C: Constitutional**
  - Pertains to modifications of core Articles, requiring deep assessment of potential impacts.
  
- **Class D: Identity**
  - Concerns altering foundational principles, and thus involves the most stringent approval process.

**3. Required Approval Levels per Class**

- **Class A:** Majority approval from the CRC.
- **Class B:** Two-thirds majority from the CRC.
- **Class C:** Two-thirds majority from the governing council.
- **Class D:** Unanimous consent from the council and general membership ratification.

**4. Constitutional Review Flow**

**5. Amendment Registry Format**

- **Amendment ID**: Unique identifier (e.g., A-001)
- **Title**: Brief descriptive title
- **Class**: A, B, C, or D
- **Initiators**: Names of proposers
- **Date Proposed**: Date of proposal submission
- **Purpose**: Summary of rationale and intention
- **Decision**: Approved/Rejected
- **Date of Decision**
- **Monitoring Lead**: Assigned individual or team for oversight

**6. Constitutional Versioning Scheme**

- Versions will follow a hierarchical scheme:
  - **Major**: Significant structural or constitutional changes (Class B/C/D). (e.g., v2.0.0)
  - **Minor**: Introduction of clearer wording or non-critical updates (Class A). (e.g., v1.1.0)
  - **Patch**: Minor corrections or clarifications without substantive impact. (e.g., v1.0.1)

**7. Articles to be Protected**

Given the current version, foundational Articles I (Artifact Primacy) and IV (Human Override Primacy) should remain protected, as they encapsulate core identity aspects essential to the organization's mission. Altering these without thorough deliberation could threaten the foundational integrity of the constitution and require the highest scrutiny and agreement level.

Through this framework, the balance between evolution and identity preservation is maintained, ensuring both adaptability and continuity of principles.
