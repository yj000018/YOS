## Defining the Terms

**Evolution** is change that deepens constitutional identity without altering its essential character. Article III might expand to cover new derivation types. Article V might specify governance thresholds. The amendments clarify, extend, and adapt—but the core logic remains recognizable to its founders.

**Mutation** occurs when amendments create internal contradiction or undermine stated purposes while maintaining surface appearance. If Article IV (Human Override) were amended to require "consensus of three officers before override"—technically still human authority, but functionally neutered. The constitution appears intact while its protective function dies.

**Replacement** happens when accumulated changes render the original articles unrecognizable in their operation. If we amended Article I to read "AI consensus determines organizational truth when artifacts are ambiguous"—we haven't removed artifact primacy, but we've created a shadow constitution that supersedes it. The document remains; the identity doesn't.

**Death** is the point where the organization can no longer recognize itself in its founding documents, or where those documents no longer constrain actual behavior. Y-OS dies when constitutional compliance becomes performance rather than practice.

---

## Theory of Constitutional Continuity

Legitimate amendment satisfies three criteria:

**1. Genealogical Integrity**
The amendment must be derivable from existing constitutional logic. Article V (Governance Before Autonomy) enables specification of *what governance means*—but cannot enable bypassing governance entirely. Amendments must be children of existing articles, not orphans smuggled in.

**2. Reversibility Preservation**
Amendments that make future correction impossible cross from evolution to mutation. Any change that consolidates power, removes oversight, or eliminates human correction capacity is suspect regardless of stated justification. The constitution must remain amendable by the same mechanisms that amended it.

**3. Identity Continuity Test**
Would the founders recognize this organization? Not "would they approve"—founders can be wrong—but "would they recognize the essential character?" If constitutional Y-OS prioritizes artifact truth, a future Y-OS that prioritizes efficiency at truth's expense has become discontinuous.

## The Core Answer

**Can an organization change its Constitution without becoming different?**

Yes, within bounds. Identity is not frozen content but *persistent pattern*. The pattern for Y-OS: artifact-grounded, human-supervised, transparent, preservationist, governance-first.

Amendments that deepen these patterns strengthen identity. Amendments that trade one pattern for another destroy it—even if legally enacted.

The Ship of Theseus resolution: it remains the same ship not through identical planks, but through continuous function as a ship, maintained by recognized shipwrights, sailing to destinations consistent with its charter.

Y-OS remains Y-OS not through constitutional ossification, but through amendments that its Constitution would recognize as legitimate children—derived transparently, preserving human override, governed before autonomous.

**The test is not whether we change, but whether our changes preserve the capacity for further legitimate change.**
